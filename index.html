NYC Public Housing & Hurricane Risk

When Evacuation
Is Uneven

Mapping hurricane risk and evacuation access gaps
for NYCHA developments in New York City

By Allison Han  ·  April 2026

Scroll to explore ↓
"In New York City, hurricane risk is not only about
where water reaches. It is also about
who can realistically get to safety."

When hurricane risk is mapped in New York City, the focus usually falls on the coastline, flood zones, or evacuation areas. But for residents of public housing, another question matters just as much: when a storm is approaching, how easy is it to reach a designated evacuation center?

Public housing is not a marginal part of the city's housing landscape. NYCHA, the New York City Housing Authority, operates hundreds of developments spread across all five boroughs, housing hundreds of thousands of residents who may face additional challenges during evacuation: older adults, people with disabilities, and households with fewer transportation options.

This project asks a simple but important planning question: which NYCHA developments face the greatest evacuation disadvantage during hurricanes? Evacuation disadvantage means a development is located in a higher hurricane risk area and farther from a designated evacuation center.

Step 1 of 4

NYCHA developments are deeply tied to the city's hurricane risk geography

The first step was to place NYCHA developments against New York City's hurricane evacuation zones. This map establishes the broad spatial pattern: NYCHA is not evenly distributed across the city's hazard landscape.

A noticeable share of developments are located in coastal or low-lying parts of the city, especially along southern Brooklyn, parts of Queens, Lower Manhattan, and Staten Island.

This matters because it shows that hurricane preparedness for NYCHA is not a niche problem affecting only a few isolated sites. Public housing is woven into the geography of coastal exposure itself.

Step 2 of 4

Exposure does not automatically mean access

To move from hazard exposure to evacuation planning, I mapped designated hurricane evacuation centers and created a 0.5-mile buffer around them as a proxy for nearby access. I then identified NYCHA developments located outside that buffer.

Some developments may already sit in areas with meaningful hurricane exposure, yet still fall outside a reasonable proximity threshold to an official evacuation center. In those places, the burden of reaching safety shifts more heavily onto residents themselves.

The pattern here suggests that evacuation infrastructure is not evenly aligned with where vulnerable housing is located.

Step 3 of 4

The problem becomes sharper when social vulnerability is added

Distance alone does not tell us which access gaps matter most. A development outside the buffer may cause limited inconvenience in one district, but a much more serious burden in another if residents are also more socially vulnerable.

The most urgent areas are not simply the farthest places from evacuation centers. They are the places where spatial access gaps and social vulnerability overlap. In those districts, evacuation disadvantage is not just about distance, it reflects a broader set of structural pressures that can make emergency response harder, slower, and more unequal.

What stands out most is that these overlaps are not randomly scattered. They cluster in specific parts of the city, especially along vulnerable waterfront districts and parts of the Bronx.

Step 4 of 4

From spatial pattern to planning priority

To make the analysis more actionable, I translated the overlap pattern into a set of top-priority areas. Planning decisions are rarely made at the scale of an abstract citywide surface, they are made through districts, agencies, capital priorities, emergency coordination, and neighborhood-level outreach.

The resulting pattern points to a citywide issue with very uneven local expression. Several of the highest-priority areas are concentrated in southern Brooklyn, southeast Queens, the South Bronx, and Staten Island , places where planning investment and outreach are most urgently needed.

Evacuation inequity is not an isolated exception. It is built into the city's geography in ways that planning needs to confront directly.

How the analysis was built

Data Sources

Three public datasets from NYC Open Data: NYCHA Public Housing Developments, Hurricane Evacuation Centers, and Hurricane Inundation by Evacuation Zone.

Buffer Analysis

A 0.5-mile buffer was created around each evacuation center. Developments located outside this buffer were flagged as having an access gap.

Vulnerability Overlay

A Social Vulnerability Index (SVI) was summarized at the council district level to identify where access gaps overlap with broader social vulnerability.

Priority Scoring

Districts were ranked by combining NYCHA count outside buffer, mean hurricane risk zone, and mean SVI score into a composite priority score.
